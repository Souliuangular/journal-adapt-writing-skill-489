## Priority Rules

### P1 — Hard Preserve

Never change:

- citations or citation keys;
- equations, labels, references, notation, and variables;
- numerical results;
- assumptions, propositions, lemmas, and corollaries;
- author-defined terminology;
- model objects or empirical objects.

### P2 — Target-Journal Patterns

Apply reviewed JEEM patterns:

1. Frame the manuscript as a policy-design, welfare, incidence, or allocation problem.
2. Move quickly from broad climate-policy context to the specific economic mechanism.
3. Make the welfare criterion, policy instrument, or empirical identification visible early.
4. Tie contributions to model structure, data design, benchmark comparison, or mechanism.
5. Define the environment before formal derivation.
6. Organize results around benchmark comparison and mechanism interpretation.
7. Keep policy implications bounded by the paper's design.

### P3 — Secondary Corpus / Exemplar Patterns

Use optional secondary-corpus patterns only when they support, rather than override, JEEM fit:

- brief accessible motivation is acceptable in the opening;
- distributional-policy writing should separate burden, recycling, welfare, and feasibility channels;
- field-top papers can help clarify the welfare criterion or decision margin.

### P4 — Optional Static Base Skill

If the user provides a static writing skill, apply it only when corpus patterns do not decide. In this public MVP sample, no static skill is shown.

Recommended fallback behavior:

- one main claim per paragraph;
- claim before evidence;
- mechanism before implication;
- avoid generic novelty claims;
- define the economic environment before formal derivation.

### P5 — Cleanup

Remove:

- empty transitions;
- generic contribution statements;
- unsupported broad policy claims;
- AI-flavored emphasis that does not name a mechanism.

## Section Guidance

### Abstract

Use: problem -> mechanism or method -> central comparison/result -> bounded implication.

### Introduction

Use:

1. policy or welfare-design problem;
2. why the problem matters;
3. what prior work establishes;
4. unresolved mechanism or decision margin;
5. model/empirical approach;
6. main result or comparison;
7. concrete contributions;
8. roadmap.

### Literature Review

Organize by mechanism, not chronology. Each stream should end by clarifying what it leaves unresolved.

### Model / Methods

Define objects, instruments, assumptions, and welfare or empirical criteria before dense derivation.

### Results

Lead with benchmark comparison, then mechanism interpretation.

### Discussion / Conclusion

Keep implications bounded. Do not reopen the literature review.

## Cautions and Conflicts

- Secondary-corpus policy language may be broader than JEEM style; use sparingly.
- Static economics rules may favor compact introductions; JEEM allows developed introductions when each paragraph advances the mechanism.
- If PDF conversion is incomplete, retry or replace the paper before Phase 1.
